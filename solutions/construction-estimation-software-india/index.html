The bid is where the margin is won or lost

Every rupee of profit on a project is decided before a single brick is laid — in the estimate. Price too high and you lose the work; price too low and you win a job that bleeds you for months. Yet most contractors still estimate in a one-off spreadsheet built from scratch each time, with rates half-remembered and half-copied from the last quote. It's slow, it's inconsistent, and worst of all the quote is completely disconnected from how the work will later be billed.

That disconnect is the hidden cost. You quote in one spreadsheet, win the job, then rebuild a BOQ in another to bill against — and the two don't quite match, so reconciling what you promised against what you're billing becomes a recurring headache. Construction estimation software fixes both problems: it lets you estimate fast from a reusable rate base, and it carries the winning numbers straight into the project so your quote, your BOQ and your bills all speak the same language.

Quote it once, bill from the same numbers: a winning estimate becomes the project's BOQ, so there's nothing to rebuild and nothing to reconcile.

Consistency is what makes estimates profitable

The biggest estimating risk for a growing contractor isn't a single bad quote — it's inconsistency. When every estimate is rebuilt from scratch, the same item gets priced three different ways across three jobs, and you can't tell which quotes are actually making money. A reusable rate base fixes that: price your standard items once, keep them current, and every estimate starts from the same honest baseline. You still adjust for a specific job — a difficult site, a tight programme, a premium client — but you adjust from a known number rather than guessing from a blank cell.

Over time this builds something valuable: a feel for your real costs. Because your estimates and your actual bills live in the same system, you can compare what you quoted against what the work actually earned, and tighten your rates accordingly. That feedback loop — estimate, execute, compare, refine — is how contractors move from hoping a job is profitable to knowing it.

One continuous flow: estimate, BOQ, measure, bill

An estimate shouldn't be a dead end. In True Site Sync a won estimate becomes the project's BOQ, your team records measurements against those same items, and billing draws on the rates you quoted — feeding your cash-flow view as money comes in. Nothing is rebuilt between stages, which is what removes the reconciliation work and keeps the promise you made at tender connected to the money you collect at the end.

Pricing items the right way

A good rate isn't a guess — it's built from what an item actually consumes: material, labour, equipment, wastage and your margin. The contractors who win profitable work are the ones who understand their real item rates, not the ones who shave the headline number to win a bid they'll regret. True Site Sync lets you hold and reuse your own rates, so the thinking you put into pricing an item once is captured and applied every time, instead of being redone from memory on each new quote.

Reusing rates also makes your quotes defensible. When a client questions a number, you can show a consistent, itemised basis rather than a round figure pulled from nowhere. And because the same rates flow into the project's BOQ when you win, the price you justified at tender is the price you bill against — no quiet erosion of margin between the quote and the first RA bill.

Common estimating mistakes this helps you avoid

The first and most expensive mistake is inconsistent rates — pricing the same item differently across jobs so you can't tell which quotes make money. A reusable rate base removes that variance. The second is forgetting GST or applying it wrong, so the headline quote and the eventual invoice don't match and the client feels misled; here GST is applied consistently as you build the estimate.

The third is the quote-to-billing disconnect: winning the work on one spreadsheet and then rebuilding a BOQ in another, so what you promised and what you bill drift apart. Converting the estimate directly into the project's BOQ closes that gap. And the fourth is simply being too slow — losing a job because the quote took a week. Pricing from a ready rate base turns a week into an afternoon, so you bid on more work and win more of it.
